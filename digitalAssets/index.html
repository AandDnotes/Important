Digital Assets

In addition to various documents, we have quite a lot of information stored in digital form. It seems that the present-day term for these pieces of information is 'digital assets', so this page tries to list those assets and points you to their source.

Much of this digital information is of no value and most of it needs sorting out (deleting or organising). Perhaps that will happen over the coming years. However, we will look at our digital assets in the form of email, web sites, photos and videos and then look at the service providers and the various devices that we own.

Our intent is that our executors will have legal access to all these assets and there is a general clause on the subject in our wills. However, each service provider seems to have their own process for granting access.

Service Providers

When it comes to access by executors to files stored on mail servers or cloud services, each service provider takes a different approach. However, each cloud storage service stores copies of the files on the local device, so all files are readily available. These are the service providers that we use:

Google

We both have email accounts with Google as described above. We also use Google Drive, currently using just over half of our free storage allocation.

Google have an Inactive Account Manager that allows the account owner to define to what data one or more trusted contacts are given access. You also have to specify what is meant by 'inactive'. This has not yet been set up.

iCloud

I have an iCloud email account, as noted above, and iCloud Drive. At the time of writing I only have 13% of the free space available.

Apple's facility for gaining access to someone's data is a 'Legacy Contact'. One or more contacts are provided with a 'key', which, together with the death certificate, can be used to access data. This has not yet been set up.

GitHub

All our web sites are hosted on GitHub. It is possible to invite a 'successor' who, by presenting a death certificate, is given access to public repositories. Once again, this has not been set up.

Dropbox

I have a Dropbox account, but it only really stores a copy of my PhD thesis, my published book and some book ideas. Of the various cloud storage service providers, Dropbox seems to be the least friendly when it comes to executor access to data. Basically, nothing is set up beforehand, you just have to provide a lot of documentation.
